Digital Citizenship
Starter Guide

Master the three pillars of responsible internet use — literacy, safety, and communication.

📚
Digital Literacy
Find · Evaluate · Create
Verify Sources

Cross-check information across at least 3 reputable sources before sharing. Look for .gov, .edu, or established news outlets.

Spot Fake News

Check the URL carefully, look for unusual formatting, overly emotional headlines, or missing author credentials.

Search Smartly

Use quotation marks for exact phrases. Add "site:" to restrict searches. Use advanced filters to narrow by date.

Digital Footprint

Everything you post online can persist indefinitely. Think before you share — your digital reputation matters.

Copyright & IP

Respect intellectual property. Use Creative Commons licensed content and always attribute original creators.

AI Awareness

Understand that AI-generated content can contain errors. Always verify AI outputs with authoritative sources.

Online Safety
Protect · Detect · Respond
Strong Passwords

Use 12+ character passwords mixing letters, numbers & symbols. Never reuse passwords across sites. Use a password manager.

Enable 2FA / MFA

Two-factor authentication adds a vital second layer. Use an authenticator app — it's more secure than SMS codes.

Phishing Alerts

Never click suspicious links in emails or texts. Check sender addresses carefully. When in doubt, go directly to the website.

Public Wi-Fi

Avoid banking or shopping on public Wi-Fi. Use a VPN to encrypt your connection on untrusted networks.

Software Updates

Keep OS, apps and antivirus software updated. Most cyberattacks exploit known vulnerabilities in outdated software.

Privacy Settings

Audit social media privacy settings regularly. Limit what personal data apps can access on your device.

Email Etiquette
Write · Respond · Respect
Subject Lines

Write clear, specific subject lines. e.g., "Meeting Rescheduled — Mon 10 AM" not just "Meeting". This aids filtering and urgency.

Reply Time

Respond to professional emails within 24 hours. If you need more time, send a brief acknowledgement to set expectations.

CC vs BCC

CC recipients can see each other. Use BCC for mass emails or to protect recipient privacy. Avoid unnecessary CC'ing.

Tone & Language

Stay professional. Avoid slang, ALL CAPS (implies shouting), and excessive exclamation marks. Re-read before sending.

Attachments

Mention attachments in the email body. Compress large files. Prefer cloud links (Drive/OneDrive) for files over 10 MB.

Reply All

Think before using "Reply All." Ask: does everyone really need this? Unnecessary replies flood inboxes and reduce productivity.
